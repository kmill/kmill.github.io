The algebra associated to SO(1,1)

The special orthogonal group SO(2)SO(2) sits inside the space of 2×22×2 real matrices, and by taking the closure under scalar multiplication we obtain the set of all matrices of the form

[a−bba].[a−bba]. This set of matrices forms an RR-algebra, and it is isomorphic to CC by having the above matrix correspond to a+bia+bi.

In Math 54, we are covering quadratic forms right now, and I wondered what this sort of construction would give for SO(1,1)SO(1,1). Let’s set q(x)=x21−x22q(x)=x12−x22, so then a 2×22×2 matrix AA is in SO(1,1)SO(1,1) if detA=1detA=1 and q(Ax)=q(x)q(Ax)=q(x) for all x∈R2x∈R2. Let’s just solve for what form AA must have, since there’s a certain diagram I want to draw for later. Suppose

A=[acbd].A=[acbd]. Plugging in e1e1 to q(Ax)=q(x)q(Ax)=q(x) we get a2−b2=1a2−b2=1, and plugging in e2e2 we get c2−d2=−1c2−d2=−1. The quadratic form has a corresponding bilinear form ⟨x,y⟩=x1y1−x2y2⟨x,y⟩=x1y1−x2y2 satisfying ⟨x,x⟩=q(x)⟨x,x⟩=q(x), and one can show ⟨Ax,Ay⟩=⟨x,y⟩⟨Ax,Ay⟩=⟨x,y⟩. Hence, ⟨Ae1,Ae2⟩=ac−bd⟨Ae1,Ae2⟩=ac−bd and ⟨e1,e2⟩=0⟨e1,e2⟩=0, giving an additional equation. Geometrically speaking, this is that the columns of AA are orthogonal (via the dot product) after reflecting the second vector over the e1e1-axis. Both options for Ae2Ae2 can be obtained from reflections of Ae1Ae1 over the lines y=xy=x and y=−xy=−x. Hence, AA must be of the form [a±bb±a][a±bb±a] for a2−b2=1a2−b2=1. One of the options has determinant 11, and the other (the corresponding to −b−b and −a−a) has determinant −1−1. Hence, SO(1,1)SO(1,1) corresponds to symmetric matrices of determinant 11 that are also symmetric across the minor axis.

Now let’s consider the corresponding algebra, which is from taking all scalar multiples of these matrices. The effect of this is to take all matrices of the form

[abba][abba] with no constraints on aa and bb. (If I hadn’t done the above analysis, I would have been surprised to learn that these matrices form a commutative subalgebra!)

The only two-dimensional algebras over RR are R⊕RR⊕R, R[x]/(x2)R[x]/(x2), and CC, and it seems like it should be the first. (It can’t have nilpotent elements since the square of the above matrix has a2+b2a2+b2 in the (1,1)(1,1) entry, and for a similar reason it can’t be CC since it lacks periodic elements.) How do we decompose it, then?

It’s easy enough to solve for a set of minimal orthogonal idempotents. They turn out to be the orthogonal projections onto y=xy=x and y=−xy=−x:

π1=12[1111]π2=12[1−1−11]π1=12[1111]π2=12[1−1−11] These certainly have the property that π1+π2=idπ1+π2=id and π1π2=0π1π2=0 (while also being well outside of SO(1,1)SO(1,1) proper!). Thus, we have a decomposition [abba]=π1[abba]+π2[abba]=(a+b)π1+(a−b)π2[abba]=π1[abba]+π2[abba]=(a+b)π1+(a−b)π2 and furthermore an isomorphism to R⊕RR⊕R by sending such a matrix to (a+b,a−b)(a+b,a−b).

A consequence to this is that we get a sort of geometric rule for multiplication of matrices in SO(1,1)SO(1,1). We regard a matrix in SO(1,1)SO(1,1) as being a point on the hyperbola x2−y2=1x2−y2=1. Step 1: take each point and project them onto the lines y=xy=x and y=−xy=−x. Step 2: think of these two lines as being RR (with (12,12)(12,12) and (12,−12)(12,−12) as the units) and multiply the correspond numbers. Step 3: “un-project” the resulting two numbers to form a new point on the hyperbola. (Or said more simply, rotate the plane by π/4π/4 while scaling by 22, do component-wise multiplication of the points, then rotate and scale back. The fact that SO(1,1)SO(1,1) is closed under multiplication from this point of view is that if you have pairs (c,d)(c,d) and (e,f)(e,f) with cd=1cd=1 and ef=1ef=1, then (ce,df)(ce,df) has cedf=1cedf=1.)

For example, since points of the positive sheet of the hyperbola have the parameterization (cosht,sinht)(cosh⁡t,sinh⁡t), then under the assumption that this is supposed to be a homomorphism R→SO(1,1)R→SO(1,1) with tt behaving like an angle, we can recover some identities. As a Clifford algebra

Another way to write R⊕RR⊕R is as R[x]/(x2−1)R[x]/(x2−1), where the isomorphism from R[x](x2−1)R[x](x2−1) is from [p]↦(p(1),p(−1))[p]↦(p(1),p(−1)) with p∈R[x]p∈R[x]. The corresponding isomorphism from the above algebra to R[x]/(x2−1)R[x]/(x2−1) is given by

[abba]↦a+bx[abba]↦a+bx so xx corresponds to the permutation matrix of order 22. Hence, the algebra is isomorphic to the Clifford algebra on a 1-dimensional space with a positive-definite quadratic form. (These are also known as the split-complex numbers.)
