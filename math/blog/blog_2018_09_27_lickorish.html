The Lickorish-Wallace theorem (notes)

These are an elaboration of my notes for a pair of talks given for the Low-Dimensional Topology Student Seminar at UC Berkeley, Fall 2018.

This material is a combination of what is in Roflsen[1] Section TODO, Lickorish[2] Section TODO, and things I’ve picked up along the way.

What Lickorish and Wallace independently proved in 1960 is that every closed orientable 3-manifold bounds a compact orientable 4-manifold—so the cobordism group of closed oriented 3-manifolds is trivial. Lickorish’s method is to use a Heegaard splitting of the manifold, decompose the gluing homomorphism into a product of Dehn twists using the Lickorish twist theorem (relative to an S^3 gluing), then do integral Dehn surgeries in one of the handlebodies to obtain S^3. It then follows that the 3-manifold is the boundary of a 4-ball with attached 2-handles, one per Dehn surgery.

It’s convenient enough to assume we are working in the piecewise linear manifold category.

1. Heegaard splittings

A handlebody X is a 3-manifold homeomomorphic to a 0-handle (a D^0\times D^3) with attached 1-handles (D^1\times D^2), with the attachments along each S^0\times D^2\subset D^1\times D^2. The homeomorphism type is characterized in many ways: by the genus of \partial X, by the number of attached handles, or by the maximal number of properly embedded[3] disks such that the complement of a regular neighborhood of the disks is homeomorphic to D^3. All of these are the genus of the handlebody.

We are going to care about gluing handlebodies together, and it can be difficult to keep the data for the surface homeomorphism straight. Let \Sigma_g denote some fixed closed genus-g surface. A marking of the boundary of a handlebody X is a homeomorphism \Sigma_g\to \partial X — this sort of gives a coordinate system for the boundary of the handlebody. We will show that X can be given, up to homeomorphism, by a collection of g disjoint simple closed curves on \Sigma_g.[4]

Theorem. Let X, Y be two handlebodies and let \mathscr{C}\subset X and \mathscr{D}\subset Y be collections of properly embedded disks such that in each case the complement of a regular neighborhood of the disks is homeomorphic to a ball. If h:\partial X\to \partial Y is a homeomorphism such that h(\partial\mathscr{C})=\partial\mathscr{D}, then h extends to a homeomorphism X\to Y.

Proof. Homeomorphisms of circles extend to disks, and one can extend h to a homeomorphism on collar neighborhoods of the disks. The complements are balls, and since homeomorphisms of spheres extend to balls, one can extend h to X\to Y. ∎

Given a marking of a handlebody, one can take the inverse image in \Sigma_g of the boundaries of the disks from the theorem. These g disjoint simple closed curves are the characteristic curves of the marking, and they specify the homeomorphism type of a marked handlebody (non-uniquely). Isotopy of the characteristic curves extends to an isotopy of the disks, so characteristic curves need only be considered up to isotopy.

Definition. A Heegaard splitting of a closed orientable 3-manifold M is a pair of handlebodies X and Y along with a homeomorphism h:\partial X\to \partial Y such that M is homeomorphic to X\cup_h Y= X\amalg Y/(x \sim h(x),x\in \partial X). Intrinsically, a Heegaard splitting is a closed oriented surface S\subset X such that the complement of a regular neighborhood of S is the disjoint union of two handlebodies.

Definition. A genus-g Heegaard diagram is two sets of characteristic curves on \Sigma_g. That they are characteristic curves means they come from a marked handlebody, so a Heegaard diagram corresponds to a 3-manifold with a particular Heegaard splitting. Heegaard diagrams tend to be drawn with the two sets of curves in different colors.

Example. A Heegaard diagram for S^3 is simply \Sigma_0=S^2 with no characteristic curves, and Alexander’s lemma implies this is the only genus-0 Heegaard splitting. There are diagrams for S^3 with arbitrary genus.

Theorem. Every compact oriented 3-manifold has a Heegaard splitting.

Proof. Take a triangulation of a 3-manifold M (exists due to Moise and Bing). A regular neighborhood of the 1-skeleton (technical detail: neighborhood exists as a subcomplex in the second barycentric subdivision of the triangulation) gives a handlebody X — the vertices give 0-handles and the edges give 1-handles, and a spanning tree of 1-handles can be contracted into one 0-handle to get a handlebody. The complement Y=\operatorname{cl}(M-X) is a handlebody, too. One way to see this is to view the intersection of each 3-cell with Y as a 0-handle and a collar neighborhood of each 2-cell in Y as a 1-handle. To appease Michael, one can also say that the intersection of each 2-cell with Y gives properly embedded disks in Y, the complement of which is a disjoint union of balls. (Lemma: any compact oriented 3-manifold with a collection of disjoint properly embedded disks, whose complement is a disjoint union of balls, is a handlebody.) ∎
