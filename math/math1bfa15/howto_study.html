How to study mathematics

How do I study mathematics? There are multiple ways to address this question. What should I study? How should I study it? What should I do to be successful in mathematics classes? Or, interpreting the question as one from exasperation, how does one study mathematics? Or, from professional interest, how do mathematicians create and study mathematics? And what is this “mathematics” which is being studied, anyway?

Like anything involving modal verbs, there is no clear-cut answer to the question. The journey you take is a personal one, though I have some suggestions which might be helpful: ways to find the path and know whether you are on it, so to speak.

1. What is mathematics?

A good first thing for us to do is to get some working definition of what mathematics even is. Otherwise, how do we know when we are on the right track? At risk of being too general, I will just claim that mathematics is about finding and understanding the relationships between (abstract) things. If there are things, and you notice a pattern or relationship between them, then you have just done some mathematics.

For instance, numbers measure distances between things, magnitudes

In elementary and high school, you learn about numbers, functions, polynomials, graphs, equations and how to solve them, measuring triangles, basic geometry, and assorted other topics. In calculus, you learn about rates of change, limits, integrals, series, and some applications. Much of the time is spent learning how to compute.

Mathematics both is and isn’t about solving problems. Unsolved problems are a major drive behind the development of mathematics as a subject, but writing down a solution to an equation isn’t the goal. Rather, it is to understand the solution: why there is one, why it couldn’t be something else, what is the theory behind the problem, what other problems are like this one, and can the understanding be generalized to other situations.

2. Psychology

One interesting thing about mathematics, being pursuit of the mind, is that is an excellent background against which to observe mental processes.

Frustration and confusion are a normal part of mathematical life. If frustration and confusion are a 60W lightbulb, then mathematicians are moths.

These are unpleasant feelings, and so there is some natural aversion to things which cause the feelings. However, try to notice when this is happening, because this indicates that there is something you do not understand. Target that.

Try to know both what you know and what you do not know. The latter lets you have some focus on what you need to learn or figure out. However, be well aware that there is much you do not know you do not know. Your goal is to discover those things. Develop techniques for ferreting this stuff out.

3. Logic

Many students find their logic skills to be weak, and those who do not should really realize how weak their logic skills are so they know they should do something about it.

Get to know implication: If it is raining, there are puddles on the ground outside my house. If I see puddles on the ground outside my house, it might be raining, but it might just be my neighbor Bob who likes to wash his car. If there aren’t puddles on the ground outside my house, then it couldn’t be raining. Swap “the series converges” for “it is raining” and “the sequence of terms in the series converges to zero” for “there are puddles on the ground outside my house,” then you have the divergence test for series.

Get to know “if” and “only if”. If it is raining, there are puddles. There are puddles if it is raining. It is raining only if there are puddles. Only if there are puddles, it is raining.

Get to know vacuous truth. Mathematicians consider the following to be true: “If Evans Hall is currently a million stories tall, then humans do not need to eat or sleep.”

Get to know what “or” means. While colloquially “would you like the apple or the orange with your breakfast?” means you may have either an apple or an orange but not both, in mathematical English it might mean both (though, with this particular question, the answer could be either “yes” or “no”).
